# ProtocolLab Brand Creative Brief

## Objective

Create a recognizable, technically credible product identity for ProtocolLab
that works in a GitHub repository, at `lab.incursa.com`, in documentation,
favicons, avatars, report imagery, and social previews.

The identity should feel like a calibrated instrument: precise, calm,
inspectable, and honest about its limits. It should not feel like a generic
developer tool, a cybersecurity product, a speed contest, or a chemistry lab.

## Audience

- protocol implementers and maintainers
- performance and interoperability engineers
- lab and test-system operators
- reviewers comparing public evidence
- contributors consuming schemas, scenarios, packages, and report contracts

## Positioning

Protocol measurement becomes difficult to review when every lab invents its
own scenario names, load terms, report fields, and evidence rules. ProtocolLab
provides public, implementation-neutral contracts for describing what was
selected, what was measured, and what a result may claim.

The differentiated value is not a promise that every result is comparable.
It is the discipline of preserving the inputs, provenance, evidence class,
warnings, unsupported states, and comparability boundaries needed to judge a
result honestly.

### Approved Message Spine

- Product descriptor: **Public contracts for protocol measurement evidence.**
- Short brand line: **Shared contracts. Inspectable evidence.**
- Longer explanation: **ProtocolLab defines shared documents for describing
  what was selected, what was measured, and what a result may claim.**
- Social-card headline: **Protocol measurement with claims tied to evidence.**
- Social-card eyebrow: **CONTRACT -> RUN -> EVIDENCE**
- Attribution: **PROTOCOLLAB / BY INCURSA**

Do not use `certified`, `official benchmark`, `verified performance`,
`industry standard`, `production ready`, or `fair comparison` as brand claims.
Those phrases imply authority or proof beyond the public contract.

## Brand Personality

- precise, not sterile
- technical, not cryptic
- confident, not promotional
- transparent, not defensive
- operational, not futuristic
- evidence-first, not speed-first

## Brand Architecture And Naming

- Use `ProtocolLab` as one word with a capital `P` and `L`.
- Use the product mark with the `ProtocolLab` wordmark for the primary lockup.
- Provide an endorsed lockup with `BY INCURSA` as a subordinate line.
- Keep the Incursa corporate logo separate. The Incursa `n` is not the
  ProtocolLab icon.
- Do not add `Incursa` ahead of the product name in the primary wordmark.

## Recommended Mark Direction: Measurement Gate

The mark should show protocol traffic passing through a declared measurement
boundary.

Build the symbol from two parallel horizontal lanes that pass through a
central calibration gate or aperture. Use one or two simple packet forms or
registration ticks to make the measurement idea legible. The outer silhouette
must remain approximately square and recognizable in one color.

The metaphor is:

1. protocol lanes enter under declared conditions;
2. a controlled gate establishes the measurement boundary;
3. evidence exits in an aligned, reviewable form.

The core mark must remain simple enough to redraw from a small geometric grid.
It must still read at 16, 24, and 32 pixels without relying on tiny labels,
multiple hairlines, glow, shadows, or gradients.

Produce up to three black-and-white explorations within this direction, then
recommend one. The alternatives should vary the gate/lane geometry, not switch
to unrelated metaphors.

### Distinction Requirements

The chosen mark must not resemble:

- the Incursa `n` arch;
- the SpecTrace E3 bracket, diamond junction, horizontal trace, and terminal
  evidence tile;
- a flask, beaker, atom, globe, generic connected-node diagram, shield,
  checkmark, speedometer, rocket, or trophy;
- a protocol-specific wire symbol that would make the product appear limited
  to HTTP/3 or QUIC.

A checkmark or seal is specifically prohibited because it suggests
certification. A race/speed symbol is prohibited because ProtocolLab preserves
comparability limits instead of declaring a universal winner.

## Color Direction

Use a small, flat-color system for the identity. Core marks must work without
gradients.

| Role | Name | Value | Guidance |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Product primary | Protocol Teal | `#0F766E` | Primary mark and actions; passes normal-text contrast on white. |
| Product deep | Deep Teal | `#115E59` | Darker one-color option and hover/emphasis. |
| Product ink | Lab Ink | `#0F172A` | Wordmark, dark background, and high-contrast text. |
| Technical accent | Signal Cyan | `#0F8BA0` | Diagrams and large accents only; do not use for small text on white. |
| Product pale | Measurement Mist | `#E0F4F1` | Avatar and diagram background. |
| Neutral | White | `#FFFFFF` | Reverse marks and clean surfaces. |
| Corporate endorsement | Incursa Indigo | `#2B2E69` | Incursa endorsement only, not the ProtocolLab primary mark. |

Provide full-color, one-color dark, and one-color white versions. Test the
identity on white, Lab Ink, Measurement Mist, and an unstyled transparent
surface.

## Typography

- Wordmark and display: IBM Plex Sans SemiBold or Bold as the starting point.
- Technical labels and eyebrow copy: IBM Plex Mono Medium.
- Body/UI context: IBM Plex Sans.

The final public SVG lockups must convert wordmark lettering to outlines so
they do not depend on a locally installed font. Preserve an editable private
master with live type and record the font names and licenses. Do not bundle
font software into the public brand directory merely to make the lockup work.

The producer may customize the `ProtocolLab` lettering, but it must remain
immediately readable. Avoid stencil cuts, pseudo-code punctuation, angle
brackets around the name, and excessive monospace styling.

## Composition Language

Supporting graphics may use parallel lanes, calibration ticks, bounded cells,
small packet shapes, and sparse measurement grids. They should resemble an
instrument panel or evidence registry, not a decorative circuit board.

Use generous negative space and one clear focal point. Data-like graphics must
be abstract; do not invent throughput values or charts that could be mistaken
for published results.
